## Core Claim

RSEs in dedicated research software groups show consistently lower burnout risk than those embedded as isolated individuals within research teams or departments — a gap that has persisted across every survey wave from 2017 to 2022.

## Narrative

The primary burnout advantage of a dedicated RSE group is that professional identity, peer support, and institutional visibility are structurally reinforced rather than left to chance. In embedded models, an individual RSE often lacks colleagues who share their technical frame of reference, must advocate for their own recognition without organisational backing, and can find themselves peripheral to both the research team they support and any wider RSE community. This isolation is not merely a social inconvenience — it is a substantive driver of the conditions that precede burnout.

The composite burnout risk score — constructed from low satisfaction, high turnover intent, low supervisor recognition, and willingness to accept an equivalent job elsewhere, each normalised to a 0–1 scale — captures this across four international survey waves (2016 UK-only; 2017, 2018, and 2022 international). In every year from 2017 onward, embedded RSEs score higher burnout risk than dedicated-group RSEs by approximately 0.06 points: dedicated 0.330 vs embedded 0.391 in 2017 (p = 0.001); dedicated 0.307 vs embedded 0.368 in 2018 (p < 0.001); dedicated 0.279 vs embedded 0.342 in 2022 (p < 0.001). The 95% bootstrap confidence intervals on this difference are narrow and consistent — [0.027, 0.094], [0.033, 0.089], and [0.035, 0.092] respectively — indicating a stable, replicable signal rather than a one-off result. The 2016 UK-only wave, with a substantially smaller embedded sample (n = 86), yields a non-significant difference (p = 0.565), most plausibly reflecting statistical power rather than a genuine absence of effect.

Notably, absolute burnout risk has fallen for both groups over the period — dedicated RSEs from 0.375 in 2016 to 0.279 in 2022, embedded from 0.392 to 0.342 — suggesting that the RSE community as a whole has benefited from growing institutional recognition and a more established professional identity. The relative gap, however, has not closed.

The component-level breakdown reveals that the gap is broad-based: in 2017–2022, all four components show embedded RSEs at greater disadvantage, with turnover intent and recognition showing the largest individual contributions. This points to relational and cultural factors — feeling seen and supported within a professional community — as the primary drivers, with direct implications for where institutional effort is best directed.

For decision-makers, consolidating dispersed embedded roles into a central RSE function is supported as a staff welfare measure with a consistent, longitudinally replicated effect on burnout risk. The sustained nature of the gap across three successive international surveys strengthens the case that this is a structural rather than incidental phenomenon.
